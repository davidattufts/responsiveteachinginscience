What happens to the puddle?

Getting started

Throughout this case study (and this website) you might want to watch videos more than once to truly delve into the student ideas and teacher moves. A transcript is available for each case study page. You might want to print transcripts for reference and note-taking. In discussions, lines numbers on the transcripts facilitate referring to specific parts of the video.

Before starting the water cycle exploration, Bonnie initially worried that the launching question would not elicit much response from students, but was prepared to press students for deeper thinking. Bonnie suspected that students would say a quick "evaporation!" to the puddle question, so she planned to respond by asking what students meant by evaporation. The first video shows the beginning of the class discussion.


Video 1

Examining the video. In lines 16 to 25 of the transcript, Bonnie launches her effort to get students to think beyond a quick answer to the puddle question. It's interesting to note that Bonnie does not ask for a definition of evaporation. What does she do instead? What are some other options she could pursue?

Beau's response in line 31 of the transcript opened a door to going beyond evaporation as the explanation. He said, " … something caused the puddle to turn to water vapor … " Bonnie responded to Beau's idea by encouraging a discussion of what caused the puddle to turn to vapor. In addition to being responsive to Beau, Bonnie encouraged the class to attend to the cause of the evaporation.

 

Ideas begin to flow

The following video shows the class becoming comfortable with taking intellectual risks. Hannah begins by implying a common perception, i.e., that when doing science, we must use scientific terms. But, with encouragement from Bonnie, Hannah moves past that roadblock and is able to explain her idea of sections of water floating up from the puddle. This became an important idea for Hannah and was revisited several times in the unit.


Video 2

Reflecting on the video. What are some ways to make students comfortable with taking intellectual risks? How is intellectual risk-taking in alignment with the practices of science?

 

Puddle size and evaporation

The next video was recorded about 30 minutes into the discussion. Jacob and Hannah sensed that the size of the puddle affects its evaporation. A superficial explanation might be, "more water requires more time to evaporate." Given the opportunity, though, Hannah and Jacob were more thoughtful. In their reasoning, they mention the role of the puddle edges and how bits of water might rise.


Video 3

Examining the video. What are Hannah and Jacob's ideas? How do the ideas seem similar, and how do they seem different? How are the ideas scientific? What questions would you want to ask Jacob and Hannah to help you better understand their ideas?

 

Next Moves

So far students had been offering their ideas and reasoning about what happened to the puddle. Bonnie had been listening to their ideas and asking questions to better understand them. Throughout the discussion Bonnie had been making instructional decisions such as who to call on and what kinds of additional information to pursue. We call her actions 'next moves.' A next move is an instructional response to students' ideas.

Two noteworthy next moves thus far in the class discussion were Bonnie's decision to follow up on Beau's mention of a cause for evaporation, and Bonnie's attention to Hannah's idea of sections of water rising from the puddle.

The video below shows another example of a next move. To keep track of discussion, Bonnie is an avid note-taker. She has a journal that she writes in as the students talk. This helps her keep up with the ideas in the class, and underscores how she values the students and their thinking. In the dialogue below, Bonnie uses the notes in her journal to frame her last move for this session.


Video 4

Reflecting on the video. Bonnie's next move was to ask students to make a list of the information they would need to answer the puddle question. Bonnie's next move was just one of many equally valid next moves that a responsive teacher could pursue. What are some other next moves that a teacher could have made?
