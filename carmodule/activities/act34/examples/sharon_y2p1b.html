Summary for Days 14-16

Students spent three more days on this module. The last three days focused on how putting different materials (jelly, jam, oil, etc.) on a ramp would affect the speed of a toy car going down the ramp.
 

Day 14

Jamir begins by showing his insight from the previous day, that the (rubber) doorstop does not slide down a ramp, whereas a round object (car with wheels) will slide down. Students discuss why a doorstop does not slide down ramp. [It does slide down when the ramp is very steep.] Kyleigh says it’s because of the shape (slant?) of the ramp. Jourdan disagrees, saying it’s because the rubber prevents it from sliding. Others agree. There is a debate about slope (our word, not theirs) versus rubber. They discuss whether which side of the rubber doorstop touched the surface mattered.

A student brings up ‘free will.’ Sharon talks about that, and asks if a car or doorstop has free will. Students say ‘no,’ so Sharon asks what makes them slide down a ramp. Jourdon says the car has free will when going down the ramp. If you push a car, however, it doesn’t have free will. Alexis says that the car on a ramp does not have free will—it didn’t decide between staying or sliding down; it had to slide down because there is nothing to hold it. Pricilla says the angle matters (angle of the slide). Jennifer says, “The higher it is, the faster it goes.”

  • The students grapple with interesting and complex substance here. Jourdan argues that a car has free will because when you put it down a ramp, it goes by itself without anything pushing it. Isaac talks about how wheels of a car turn and push the car forward (it is not clear whether the wheels or the carpet pushes the car forward). He says the car stops because the wheels ‘get tired’ (they originally get energy, but don’t get any more). In remote control cars, when you push a button the car is getting energy from the battery (electricity). Other students ask interesting questions. Isaac seems to say that when the axle turns, there is scratching (friction), and that is what causes a car to stop.

  • Isaac describes a mechanism for how the car moves. As the wheels turn, each part is pressed against the floor, pushing the car forward. Then the next part of the turning wheel pushes the car further, and so forth. Without the wheels, the car would just drag to a stop. When asked if the car would then move continuously forever, Isaac says that the car does stop because the wheels get 'tired.' When Scarlett challenges his answer by asking how can wheels get 'tired,' Isaac then provides a mechanistic reason for why the car slows down. He says that: (1) there are strings that scratch the wheels, causing them to turn slower and slower (car slowing down); and (2) the car does not get any electricity to give it more energy, unlike a remote control car that gets bursts of energy from a battery when you push a button.

     

Day 15

Sharon mentions all the materials she has brought for students to use for their experiments exploring cars going down ramps with different materials on the ramp. Which material would allow the car to go faster (oil, jelly, jam, foil, wax paper)? She also mentions other things they could do today: explore which things (e.g., different kinds of shoes) would go down the ramp faster or slower; food and energy.


Video Clip 1 (Day 15)

Kyleigh predicts that jam on the ramp will make a car go slower (because the wheels would get stuck), but jelly would allow it to go faster, and she wants to test that out. Alexis and Jourdan debate about whether jelly, jam, or oil would make the car go faster. Then they do some planning for the experiments. [Sharon mentions another class needs to join them.] Pricilla shares her experiment plan. Jourdan comes up and asks questions because he doesn’t understand. Students talk about mixing some of the ingredients. There is no time to go outside today to do experiments, so Sharon asks them to write their plans for their experiments.

Day 16

The students continue to talk about their experiments. Jourdan talks about mixing something, which is sticky, with something that is slippery. Sharon mentions they’ll take three large boards outside to perform the experiment.


Video Clip 1 (Day 16)

They go outside to perform the experiments. There are three ramps. One has jelly, one has oil, and the third board is plain, but that is not initially used. They race and the car goes faster down the oil-covered ramp. Scarlett (with help from Jamir) explains why oil makes the car go faster and jelly makes it go slower. Sharon asks if the car would go faster on a plain board than on an oily board. A student makes a prediction and gives her reason. Then they repeat the experiment with all three boards. It seems the car went ‘slightly’ faster down the plain board than the oily board, but students thought it was a tie. The car going down the jelly board was much slower.

  • While doing the experiment, Scarlett immediately begins proposing a causal narrative for what might be taking place: the car goes slower on the jelly because the jelly is sticky. Jamir jumps in with another account: the jelly might be sticking in the top of the wheels, causing them to turn more slowly. The students start talking about the science before they get back to the classroom, indicating that the experiment is for the purpose of understanding how a toy car moves.
     

After the experiments, the students return to the classroom and talk about the experiment they did with jelly and oil. They discuss why the car went slower on the jelly than it did on the oil. They discuss the stickiness of the jelly along with the possibility of whether the oil drying has an impact on the speed of the car.
