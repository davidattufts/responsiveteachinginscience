Summary for Days 4–7

During the first three days of the Toy Cars implementation, Sharon's students shared many ideas for how to get a toy car to move. One idea that became the focus of a great deal of discussion was ramps, and because of this interest, Sharon decided to continue to focus on ramps over the next few days.
 

Day 4

The next day (Day 4), Sharon starts off by putting Teresa's version of the 'two-chairs' idea on the board. She asks the students to come up with predictions for what they think is going to happen and afterwards, they will try it out themselves.

Some students think the car will make it to the top of the second chair and then fall, some think it will fall in the gap between the chairs, and some think it won't even make it down the back of the first chair. Jourdan says that if the chair-back is straight, the car won't make it, but if the chair is curved, it will make it. They devote some time to problem-shooting the two-chairs idea, mostly focused on keeping the car from falling through the holes in the chair backs and the gap between the chairs.

Day 5

At the start of Day 5, Sharon asks her students to get into groups that are different than the groups they worked with the day before, and explain to each other what happened when they tested out the two-chairs idea.

In the following clip, Kyleigh explains how her group made it jump from one chair to another. She says they did two different trials: 'weak and strong.' Sharon asks her what she means by weak and strong, and Kyleigh answers that strong means 'more energy.' Sharon asks what they did to give it more energy and Kyleigh says 'it's like when you're running and it gives you more energy… because if you have energy, you go fast.' Kyleigh said to give the car more energy, she pushed it back farther before letting it go (pushed it harder). They found out that when they gave the car more energy, it made it to the other side, but when they did not give it as much energy, it fell down on the ground.


Video Clip 1 (Day 5)

After hearing the students talking about 'giving the car more strength' and thinking about what that means in terms of energy, Sharon decided that a next move should be focused on this idea. She asks them to test out the idea again, but this time they can use books and whiteboards instead of chairs. She wants them to focus on getting the car down one side of the ramp and getting it up the other side and back down again. She wants them to pay attention specifically to what role 'making the car stronger' plays in helping the car go down, up, and back down again.

Day 6

After the students spent time the previous day designing their ramps, on Day 6 Sharon engages them in a whole class discussion about what they discovered. To start the class conversation, Sharon asks Ray to draw a picture of his group's ramp configurations. Then Jose comes up and draws his group's ramp configuration. Ray's ramp is on the left and Jose's ramp is on the right. After asking the two students, Sharon wrote, "Push hard," next to each ramp.  

Then Jamir comes up to the board and compares Ray's ramp with Jose's ramp. Day 7

Day 7 starts with Sharon asking Jamir to come to the board and share his idea with the class. She projects his notebook onto the smart board, and he explains that he had an idea about Jourdan's experiment from the day before about a car going up and down a ramp he set up by leaning wood on a coffee table. He says that if the car is heavier in back, it will fall back down the hill, but if it is heavier in front, it will make it over the hill.


Jamir's track

Jourdan asks if he needs to put a lot of energy in the car to get it over the hill, and Sharon interjects that Jamir told her earlier that the car will make it over the hill without even pushing it. Alexis thinks that he would need to push it to get it over, and Jamir responds that he made the first hill bigger so it could make it all the way over the hill. Sharon asks the class to take a vote: who thinks the car will make it over the hill without a push and who thinks the car needs a push to make it over the hill?

Jennifer agrees with Jamir that the car would go over the hill without pushing it, and Sharon asks her why. Jennifer thinks that, like Jimmy's idea, if the first hill is taller, the car should be able to make it over the second hill. Jamir says that, just like dropping something from a distance off the ground (he drops an eraser), the car going down the hill will go fast without having to push it.

  • Jamir suggests that whether the car makes it over the second hill depends on the distribution of weight across the car: if it has more weight in the front, it will make it over the hill; if it has more weight in the back, it will fall back down the front side of the hill.
     
  • Jordan asks how the car can go down first hill, then make it up and over the second hill. He suggests that the car needs a lot of energy (initially) to make it up and over the second hill. Jamir says that because the first hill is 'bigger' and the second hill is 'small,' the car can just be let go at the top of the first hill and it will make it up and over the second hill. Jennifer later suggests the same reason for why the car will make it over the second hill. At the end Jamir says the situation is just like letting go of an eraser: it will fall by itself to the ground without you having to push it.
